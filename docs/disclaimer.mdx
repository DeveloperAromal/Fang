## Authorized Use Only

Fang is a professional-grade reconnaissance tool intended for:

- **Bug bounty programs** — targets explicitly listed in a program's scope on platforms like HackerOne, Bugcrowd, or Intigriti
- **Penetration testing engagements** — with a signed Statement of Work or Rules of Engagement document from the client
- **Security research** — on systems you own, control, or have received explicit written permission to test
- **CTF (Capture the Flag) competitions** — within the defined scope of the competition

---

## What "Authorized" Means

Before running Fang against any target, you must have:

1. **Written permission** from the system owner — verbal permission is not sufficient in most legal contexts
2. **Defined scope** — know exactly which domains, IP ranges, and systems are in scope
3. **Defined rules of engagement** — understand what types of testing are permitted and any time restrictions

If you are participating in a bug bounty program, read the program's policy page carefully. Pay attention to:

- In-scope vs out-of-scope assets
- Prohibited testing methods (e.g. DoS, physical attacks)
- Disclosure timelines
- Safe harbor clauses

## Legal Context

Unauthorized access to computer systems is a criminal offense in virtually every jurisdiction:

| Jurisdiction | Relevant Law |
|---|---|
| United States | Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) |
| United Kingdom | Computer Misuse Act 1990 |
| European Union | Directive on Attacks Against Information Systems |
| India | Information Technology Act 2000 |
| Australia | Criminal Code Act 1995, Part 10.7 |

This is not an exhaustive list. Consult a legal professional if you have questions about the laws applicable to your situation.

## Responsible Disclosure

If you discover a vulnerability using Fang, follow responsible disclosure practices:

1. **Do not exploit** the vulnerability beyond what is necessary to confirm it exists
2. **Document** your findings clearly and professionally
3. **Report** to the organization through their official security disclosure channel or bug bounty program
4. **Allow reasonable time** for the organization to patch — typically 90 days is the industry standard before public disclosure
5. **Do not disclose publicly** until the vulnerability is patched or the disclosure window has passed
